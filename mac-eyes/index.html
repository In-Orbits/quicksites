What we know, what it likely is, and what to do next

What This Likely Is

Most Likely: Acute Acquired Esotropia

Esotropia means one eye turns inward (toward the nose). "Acute acquired" means it came on suddenly rather than being present since birth. In young children, this can happen for several reasons:

  • Post-viral cranial nerve palsy (CN VI) — The sixth cranial nerve controls the muscle that pulls the eye outward. A cold or virus can cause temporary inflammation around this nerve, weakening it. The eye drifts inward because the outward-pulling muscle isn't working at full strength. This is the leading explanation given Mac's recent cold.
  • Accommodative esotropia — Linked to farsightedness. The eye crosses when trying to focus. Can present suddenly around ages 2-4, though the rapid onset here with a concurrent illness makes the post-viral explanation more likely.

Why the Cold Matters

Post-viral cranial neuropathy is well-documented in pediatric literature. The sequence is straightforward:

  1. Child gets a viral illness (cold, flu, etc.)
  2. The virus or immune response causes inflammation near cranial nerve VI
  3. The nerve temporarily can't do its job of pulling the eye outward
  4. The eye drifts inward (esotropia)
  5. As the inflammation resolves, the nerve recovers and the eye straightens

This typically resolves on its own over weeks to a few months as the inflammation clears. No surgery is usually needed.

Why the Right Eye Consistently

The fact that it's consistently the same eye (right) is actually a helpful sign. It points to a single, localized nerve or muscle issue rather than something affecting the whole system. This is consistent with a CN VI palsy on the right side.

The Encouraging Part

If this is post-viral CN VI palsy — which the cold + rapid onset + consistent right eye pattern strongly suggests — it is usually self-limiting. The crossing resolves on its own as the viral inflammation clears. Many kids recover fully without any intervention beyond monitoring. Some may temporarily need glasses or patching while the nerve heals.

What a Doctor Will Do

  • Full eye exam with dilation — Check for farsightedness and assess the structures of the eye
  • Cover/uncover test — Measure the degree and type of eye misalignment
  • Assess eye movement in all directions — Determine if a specific muscle/nerve is weak
  • Rule out other causes — In rare cases, imaging (MRI) may be ordered to rule out anything structural, though this is uncommon when a clear viral history is present

The specialist you want is a pediatric ophthalmologist (not an optometrist). They're trained specifically in childhood eye alignment issues.

When to Go to the ER Instead of Waiting

If Mac develops any of these symptoms, don't wait for a scheduled appointment:

  • Headaches or vomiting (especially in the morning)
  • Balance or coordination problems (stumbling, trouble walking)
  • One pupil noticeably larger than the other
  • Complaining of double vision or not being able to see
  • Eye pain
  • Lethargy or behavioral changes beyond his cold symptoms

These are rare but would suggest something more than a post-viral nerve issue and need immediate evaluation.

Common Questions

Will he need surgery?

Unlikely if this is post-viral. Most cases resolve on their own. Surgery is typically only considered if the esotropia persists unchanged for 6+ months after the illness resolves.

Will he need glasses?

Possibly, at least temporarily. If farsightedness is contributing (common at this age), glasses can help the eyes work together while the nerve heals. The ophthalmologist will determine this during the exam.

Could this affect his vision long-term?

The main concern with any esotropia in young children is amblyopia ("lazy eye") — where the brain starts ignoring input from the misaligned eye. This is why prompt evaluation matters. If caught and managed early (which you're doing), the risk of lasting vision impact is low.

How long until it gets better?

Post-viral CN VI palsy in children typically resolves within 3 to 6 weeks, though some cases can take a few months. The ophthalmologist will set expectations based on their exam.

Can he still do normal activities?

Yes, for the most part. He may have some depth perception issues while the eye is crossed, so watch him on stairs, playground equipment, and during sports. His brain will likely adapt quickly in the short term.
